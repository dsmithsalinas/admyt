# Admyt — Founder Story

*The why behind Admyt. Used for the About page, press, investor conversations, and the occasional moment where the origin matters. Not the face of the brand — but the heart of it.*

**A note on how this shows up:** The founder stays mostly in the background. The face of Admyt is Sage, and eventually a high schooler or college senior who embodies that energy. But the founder story is the *why* — it's what makes everything else true. Used sparingly and intentionally, it's one of the most powerful things Admyt has.

**The feeling to leave people with:** *This person gets me. Admyt was built by someone who's actually been where I am.* Less "fighting for you," more "sees you."

---

## The short version (for About pages, bios, social)

Admyt was built by someone who was the kid it's for.

A first-gen student from a low-income family in central Illinois, where the college conversation had exactly three options: the local community college, the state school one town over, or the big Big Ten school down the road. No one talked about the thousands of other schools out there. No one asked what kind of place would actually fit.

Add being a gay teenager trying to find somewhere he could be himself — on a budget that made most options feel out of reach — and the whole thing felt less like a door opening and more like a maze with no map.

He picked a school. It wasn't the right fit. He transferred home. He made it work, and he's grateful — but he always wished it had been better. Wished someone had shown him there was more out there. Wished he'd had a friend who got it.

Years later, after a decade working in college financial aid — watching students priced out of their dreams, and others pressured into someone else's — he built the thing he wished he'd had.

That's Admyt. A friend in your corner, for the kid who doesn't have one.

## The full version (for the About page or a founder's note)

I grew up first-gen, in a low-income family, in the middle of Illinois.

When it came time to think about college, the conversation was small. Our guidance counselors — stretched impossibly thin — pointed basically everyone toward one of three places: the local community college, the mid-size state school in the next town, or the well-known Big Ten school forty-five minutes away. That was the map. If a school wasn't one of those three, most of us never heard about it.

I didn't know what I didn't know. I didn't know how many schools existed, how different they could be, or that somewhere out there might actually fit me better than the obvious choices. And "fit" mattered more for me than most. I was a gay kid trying to find a place where I could finally be myself — somewhere I could breathe. On top of that, money was a real constraint, which made it feel like even the few options I had were slipping away.

I picked a school. At first it felt like freedom — I could be more myself than I'd ever been. But I still felt alone. It still wasn't my place. So I transferred back home, to that mid-size state school, and I made it work. I'm genuinely grateful for the experience and the people in it. But I always knew it could have been better. I always wished someone had shown me there was more out there — and helped me figure out where I actually belonged.

Then I spent ten years working in college financial aid. And I saw the same heartbreak from the other side, over and over.

I saw kids who found a school they loved and simply couldn't afford it — with no one to help them find the schools that were just as good, just as right, but actually within reach.

And I saw kids for whom money was no object, but who were buckling under the weight of expectation — pushed toward a certain school, a certain major, a certain version of themselves that wasn't theirs. Nobody asking what *they* wanted.

Both of those kids needed the same thing I needed back in Illinois: someone in their corner. Someone who'd been through it. Someone who could say *here's what's actually out there, here's what might fit you, here's the honest truth about this place* — without an agenda, without pressure, without a price tag.

So I built it. I built the friend I wish I'd had. His name is Sage.

Admyt isn't about getting into the "best" school. It's about finding *your* place — the one where you'll actually thrive, become who you're meant to be, and not spend four years feeling like you're in the wrong room. Because I know what that feels like. And no kid figuring out their future should have to do it alone.

That's why the **y** in Admyt stands for **you.** It always has.

Admyt was founded by a first-gen, low-income student from central Illinois whose own college search offered just three options and no one to ask — made harder by searching for a place he could be himself as a gay teenager, on a budget that ruled most schools out. ## How to use this (and how not to)

**Do:**
- Use it on the About page, in a founder's note, in press and investor conversations
- Lead with it when authenticity matters more than polish
- Let it explain the *why* behind the values (free, no sponsored schools, surfacing affordable options, fit over rank) — every value traces back to a real moment in this story
- Keep the founder in the background of day-to-day brand; let Sage and students be the face

**Don't:**
- Make the founder the face of socials or the everyday product voice
- Over-tell it — used too often, an origin story loses its power. It's a foundation, not a slogan.
- Turn the personal details into a marketing gimmick. They're real. Treat them with care.

**On the LGBTQ+ thread specifically:** This is a genuine and meaningful part of the story, and it speaks directly to the "find a place I can be myself" promise at the heart of Admyt. Share it where it's authentic and additive — it will mean a great deal to students in the same position. Let the founder decide the venues; never use it as a checkbox.
